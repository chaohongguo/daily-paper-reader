## Abstract 

Fine-tuning MLLMs for Video Temporal Grounding (VTG) often improves in-domain performance but degrades sharply under domain shift. In this work, we find that this failure is primarily driven not just by unseen query concepts, but by 

visual domain shift , which prevents the model from coupling its learned temporal

localization knowledge with its inherent entity-attention capability. To address this, we introduce EVIDENT, a parameter-efficient adaptation framework that anchors temporal grounding in the inherent entity-attention of pre-trained MLLMs by routing VTG adaptation through explicit visual entity evidence . EVIDENT consists of three components: (i) an Entity Bottleneck Adapter that transforms dense visual tokens into compact entity-level slots, (ii) an Entity-Binding Distillation loss that instills objectness priors into the semantically unstructured MLLM visual space, guiding each slot to bind to a coherent entity, and (iii) an Entity-to-eVidence gating mechanism that leverages the captured entities as evidence, steering the model to localize moments containing query-relevant entities. Together, these components enable VTG fine-tuning to rely on entity-grounded evidence rather than brittle dataset shortcuts. Experiments on cross-domain VTG benchmarks show that EVIDENT consistently improves out-of-domain robustness while preserving competitive in-domain performance with modest parameter overhead. These results suggest that entity-level grounding is an effective inductive bias for generalizable temporal localization. 

## 1 Introduction 

Video Temporal Grounding (VTG) requires localizing the temporal extent of an event described by a natural language query in an untrimmed video. While the task has been studied extensively with specialist temporal localization models such as DETR-based architectures [ 22 , 35 , 29 , 42 ], Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have recently emerged as a compelling alternative [ 15 ,40 , 11 , 44 , 33 , 51 ]. The appeal of MLLMs is not merely their scale. Through large-scale image-text and video-text pre-training, they acquire broad visual-language alignment that can associate language queries with relevant visual entities across diverse video domains. However, VTG exposes a critical adaptation paradox . Before VTG-specific fine-tuning, pretrained MLLMs exhibit coarse but domain-agnostic entity-attention capability : given a query, they can often attend to semantically relevant visual content across different visual distributions (Figure 2 (a)). Yet this zero-shot attention is too coarse for VTG, which demands exact start and end timestamps, making task-specific fine-tuning necessary [ 15 , 40 , 44 , 51 , 33 ]. However, the extremely high cost of VTG annotations limits the scale and diversity of supervision, leaving source-domain fine-tuning vulnerable to shortcut learning—including temporal location bias [ 6, 38 , 12 ], query text bias [ 6, 25 , 17 ], and appearance bias [ 4 , 39 ]. These shortcuts cause fine-tuning to rely on source-specific patterns rather than the underlying entity-attention, so the learned localization knowledge fails to inherit the MLLM’s domain-agnostic entity-attention. As a result, fine-tuning improves In-Domain (ID) localization but harms generalization under Out-of-Domain (OOD) scenarios where shortcuts no longer hold (Figure 1, Figure 2 (b)). We call this phenomenon attention-localization decoupling in OOD VTG. In this work, we find that this decoupling is primarily driven by visual domain shift . We decompose the OOD gap into two factors: a concept domain gap , which captures whether the query involves subjects or actions unseen during source training, and a visual domain gap , which measures how visually distant the OOD videos are from the source distribution. Splitting OOD samples by query-concept overlap yields only a marginal performance difference (Figure 3 (a)). In contrast, splitting them by visual similarity to the source domain yields a substantially larger gap (Figure 3 (b)). These observations indicate that OOD degradation stems primarily from visual domain shift .These findings point to a different design principle for cross-domain MLLM-based VTG: robust adaptation should anchor temporal localization in the inherent entity-attention of the pre-trained MLLM, rather than merely fitting dense visual tokens to source-domain scene context. To this end, we propose EVIDENT , a parameter-efficient framework for EVID dence-grounded ENT ity-attentive adaptation for cross-domain VTG. Our goal is to learn transferable VTG knowledge by deriving temporal grounding predictions from query-relevant visual entity evidence . Realizing this principle requires three questions: (i) how to route adaptation through entities, (ii) what makes the entities meaningful, and (iii) how to leverage this entity information as evidence for temporal localization. EVIDENT answers these questions with three key components. First, we propose an Entity Bot-tleneck Adapter (EB Adapter) , which routes intermediate MLLM dense visual tokens through a compact set of entity slots to obtain domain-invariant representations. Unlike prior works [ 46 , 7 , 24 ]that re-run instruction tuning to reshape MLLM representations into an entity-level form, our adapter design enables VTG learning grounded in entity-based visual representations at minimal cost. Second, we incorporate an Entity-Binding Distillation (EB Distillation) loss to encourage each slot to bind to 2a coherent entity. We find that the pre-trained MLLM visual space alone has entangled representations and thus struggles to encode distinct entity information (Figure 1, Figure 11). In contrast, EVIDENT trained with our EB Distillation loss captures semantically coherent entities even under domain shift, as illustrated in Figure 1. Third, to leverage the entity information captured by EB Adapter with EB Distillation as explicit visual evidence guiding temporal localization predictions, we introduce an 

Entity-to-eVidence (E2V) gating mechanism. Using the entity-attention of the pre-trained MLLM, we score each frame for the presence of query-relevant entities and modulate EB Adapter’s per-frame contribution accordingly. This injects an inductive bias that the target moment is where the query entities co-occur, enabling evidence-grounded localization under OOD shifts. We evaluate EVIDENT under cross-domain VTG settings, training on a single source dataset and evaluating on different target datasets [ 10 , 22 , 13 ]. Across standard benchmarks, EVIDENT consistently improves OOD robustness while maintaining competitive ID performance with modest parameter and memory overhead. These results support our central claim: for robust MLLM-based VTG under visual domain shift, what is needed is evidence-grounded adaptation that fully leverages the pre-trained entity-attention, not direct fitting to source-domain context. We summarize our contributions as follows: • Through systematic analyses, we find that naïvely fine-tuning MLLMs causes attention-localization decoupling —the learned temporal localization fails to inherit the pre-trained entity-attention capability—and show that it is primarily driven by visual domain shift .• We propose EVIDENT, a parameter-efficient framework for evidence-grounded entity-attentive adaptation in cross-domain VTG. Our entity bottleneck adapter with entity-binding distillation reshapes MLLM features into entity-centric representations without re-running instruction tuning, while the proposed entity-to-evidence gating leverages these entities as 

explicit visual evidence for temporal localization. • We show that our evidence-grounded VTG adaptation yields consistent OOD robustness gains on diverse cross-domain VTG settings, while maintaining competitive in-domain performance with a modest number of parameters. 

## 2 Related Work 

Video Temporal Grounding Video Temporal Grounding (VTG) aims to localize the temporal segments in an untrimmed video that correspond to a natural language query. Recent works [ 15 , 40 ]reformulate VTG as a generative task on MLLMs that produces temporal boundaries as text tokens via task-specific instruction tuning. Follow-up work refines this formulation through interleaved frame–timestamp representations [ 33 , 28 ], causal event modeling [ 11 ], grounded tuning for long videos [ 51 ], chain-of-LoRA reasoning [ 30 ], and reinforcement learning with verifiable temporal rewards [ 43 ], all primarily relying on large-scale instruction tuning. While these efforts enhance the temporal capability of MLLMs, the fine-tuning step that is inevitably required for accurate localization remains an unexamined source of risk: these methods do not directly address the OOD generalization failure that this fine-tuning induces, which is the main issue we study. 

Object-centric Learning Object-centric learning represents a scene as a composition of entity-level parts, yielding transferable representations under domain shifts. Slot attention [ 31 ] is a representative mechanism, where learnable slots iteratively compete to explain the input, and has been extended to real-world images [ 41 ] and videos [ 19 , 45 ]. Recent work brings this idea into MLLMs: Slot-VLM [ 46 ] splits video tokens into object- and event-centric slots, Slot-MLLM [ 7] combines a Q-Former with slot attention for unified multimodal generation, and CORE [ 24 ] compresses visual tokens into a compact set of slots for efficient MLLM inference, using segmentation masks from an off-the-shelf model as an object-centric prior. However, all of these approaches require training the entire vision-language pipeline from scratch, whereas EVIDENT introduces a parameter-efficient EB Adapter attachable to pre-trained MLLMs, bringing entity-centric structure while preserving the pretrained vision-language alignment.

3.1 OOD Failure Observations Pre-trained MLLMs exhibit strong entity-attention capability. We first measure the visual-text alignment of the pre-trained Qwen2.5-VL-3B [ 3] by computing the cosine similarity between visual patches and object text tokens. As shown in Figure 2 (a), the pre-trained model exhibits strong visual-text alignment across visually distinct domains (Cha. [ 10 ] and QVH [ 22 ]), with high similarity between object text tokens (e.g., ‘book’) and the corresponding visual patches at the frame level. This indicates that the inherent entity-attention ability of pre-trained MLLMs to associate language queries with relevant visual content already transfers across diverse video distributions. 

Naïve fine-tuning causes severe OOD performance degradation. While the pre-trained model shows transferable alignment, naïvely fine-tuning it on a single source dataset breaks this transferabil-ity (Figure 2 (b)). Fine-tuning on Cha. [ 10 ] produces a 19.8-point ID-OOD gap on QVH [ 22 ], and fine-tuning on QVH [ 22 ] produces a 33.4-point gap on Cha. [ 10 ]. The fine-tuned model overfits to source-domain patterns, failing to generalize to unseen domains. 

Acquired grounding capability fails to transfer. To probe the source of this collapse, we measure how strongly the fine-tuned model’s visual-to-query cross-attention shifts toward ground-truth-interval frames, relative to the zero-shot baseline (Figure 2 (c)). We focus on layers 10–18, following prior findings that visual-language interaction in VideoLLMs primarily occurs at intermediate layers [ 18 ]. Fine-tuning shifts attention toward ground-truth segments substantially on the trained domain (ID) but only marginally on the unseen domain (OOD), revealing that the learned localization does not transfer across domains. 

3.2 Domain Gap Diagnosis 

We next ask: what is the primary cause of this transfer failure? We decompose the domain gap into two sources, a concept domain gap (whether OOD samples involve query concepts unseen during training) and a visual domain gap (how visually distant OOD samples are from the source distribution), and analyze which one dominates. We provide an additional analysis of the correlation between these two gaps in the supplementary material (Section B.3). 

Concept gap does not explain OOD failure. We split the OOD test set (QVHighlights [ 22 ]) by query-concept overlap with ID (Charades-STA [ 10 ]), separating samples whose query concepts appear in the source dataset from those that do not. As shown in Figure 3 (b), the most similar 20% of OOD samples achieve 52.8 R1@0.5, while the most dissimilar 20% drop to 39.1—a 13.7-point gap that scales with visual distance. This identifies the visual domain gap as the primary cause of OOD performance degradation. 

The model ignores visual content under domain shift. To verify whether the model genuinely grounds its predictions in visual content, we inject Gaussian noise into either the ground-truth (G.T.) interval or a random non-G.T. interval of the same length and measure R1@0.7 drop. As shown in Figure 3 (c), on ID, G.T. perturbation causes a much larger drop than random (17.4% vs. 9.6%), while on OOD the two are nearly identical (12.6% vs. 12.1%)—the model no longer attends to the actual visual content under distribution shift. Conversely, injecting noise into the entire non-G.T. interval improves performance on both ID and OOD (+13.1% and +4.6%). It suggests that explicitly steering the model toward the visual content of the G.T. interval recouples its learned localization with the pre-trained entity-attention disrupted by visual domain shift, yielding gains even under OOD.

4.3 Entity Binding Distillation 

While the EB Adapter encourages decomposition through its bottleneck structure, the bottleneck alone is insufficient: the entangled visual space of the naïve MLLM lacks the entity-level structure for slots to bind to. As a result, slots fail to specialize and produce near-uniform attention weights over visual tokens, as shown in Figure 11 (see Section D.1). To address this, we employ an Entity Binding Distillation (EB Distillation) loss. Inspired by prior works on object-centric learning [ 41 , 1], our loss distills the objectness prior from a self-supervised vision model to encourage semantically coherent slot formation, as illustrated in Figure 4 (b). 

Entity-level Matching. We extract DINOv2 [ 37 ] patch features from T frames, apply 2 × 2 average pooling to match the MLLM’s spatial resolution, and ℓ2-normalize along the feature dimension. We then perform K-means clustering ( K = Ns) on each frame independently, yielding a binary cluster map C ∈ { 0, 1}T ×K×N , where Ct,k,n = 1 indicates that patch n in frame t belongs to cluster k. Since slot and cluster indices are arbitrary, we perform per-frame Hungarian matching [ 20 ] between the normalized slot attention maps ˆA ∈ [0, 1]T ×K×N and C, using patch-averaged BCE as the cost, to obtain an optimal one-to-one matching π(t) : {1, . . . , K} → { 1, . . . , K}.

## 5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup Training & Evaluation. We build on Qwen2.5-VL-Instruct [ 3] (7B) as the backbone MLLM and fine-tune it on two source datasets, Charades-STA (Cha.) [ 10 ] and QVHighlights (QVH) [ 22 ]. We evaluate the models on three target datasets: Cha., QVH, and DiDeMo [ 13 ]. Thanks to the parameter-efficient design of EVIDENT, only the EB Adapter and LoRA modules are trainable, amounting to approximately 201M parameters ( 2.36% of the 7B backbone ). We denote each setting by its source-target pair (e.g., Cha. → QVH), reporting In-Domain (ID) performance when source equals target and Out-of-Domain (OOD) performance otherwise. All results are reported using the standard moment retrieval metrics R1@0.3, R1@0.5, R1@0.7, and mIoU. 

Baselines. We compare EVIDENT against three categories of methods. (1) Large-scale instruction-tuned MLLMs for temporal grounding, evaluated in a zero-shot manner without any task-specific fine-tuning: HawkEye [ 44 ], TimeSuite [ 51 ], UniTime [ 28 ], and VideoMind [ 30 ]. (2) DETR-based specialists trained on a single source dataset: EaTR [ 16 ] and CG-DETR [ 34 ]. (3) MLLM-based methods fine-tuned on a single source dataset: Chrono [ 33 ] with a BLIP-2 [ 26 ] backbone, and Qwen2.5-VL [3] baseline fine-tuned with LoRA [14] under the same training setup as ours. 

5.2 Results Cross-domain VTG performance. Table 1 summarizes the results. EVIDENT consistently improves OOD performance across all source-target configurations while maintaining competitive ID perfor-mance. Among MLLM-based methods, we observe two failure modes that support our motivation presented in Section 1. First, zero-shot MLLMs, even those that are large-scale instruction-tuned, remain substantially below fine-tuned methods in the ID setting, confirming that task-specific adapta-tion is necessary for precise temporal localization. Second, naïve source-domain fine-tuning suffers severely under domain shift: although Qwen2.5-VL [ 3 ] reaches strong ID performance, its OOD R1@0.5 drops by over 23.9 points on Cha. →QVH and over 19.6 points on QVH →Cha. These results demonstrate that entity-grounded visual evidence provides a robust pathway to fully leverage the entity-attention capabilities of pretrained MLLMs under domain shift.

## 6 Conclusions 

We presented EVIDENT , a parameter-efficient framework for cross-domain Video Temporal Ground-ing that anchors temporal localization in the inherent entity-attention of pre-trained MLLMs. Our analysis identifies attention-localization decoupling as a key failure in OOD VTG, which is primarily driven by visual domain shift rather than unseen query concepts. EVIDENT addresses this through three coupled components: an EB Adapter for routing visual tokens into entity-level slots, an EB Distillation loss for coherent slot bindings, and an E2V gating mechanism for entity-attention–based evidence grounding. Experiments on cross-domain VTG benchmarks show that EVIDENT consis-tently improves OOD robustness while maintaining competitive ID performance. Our results highlight that evidence-grounded entity-attentive adaptation, rather than direct fitting to source-domain context, is the key to robust cross-domain VTG.

## A Extended Related Work 

DETR-based VTG Specialists Prior VTG works follow the DETR paradigm [ 5] dominantly, where Moment-DETR [ 22 ] first cast moment retrieval as a set prediction problem and established the QVHighlights [ 22 ] benchmark. Subsequent models extend this paradigm by injecting VTG-specific inductive biases into the decoder, such as query-dependent representations [ 35 ], event-aware attention [16], and correlation-guided calibration [34]. 

Bias in Video Understanding Dataset bias has been extensively investigated across various video understanding tasks. In action recognition, Choi et al. [ 9] show that models often rely on scene context as a shortcut, achieving high accuracy without truly recognizing the action itself. Li et al. [ 27 ]formalize the notion of representation bias in video datasets and propose the Diving48 benchmark to address it. Bae et al. [ 2] further tackle this issue by learning disentangled representations of action and scene. Beyond action recognition, Lei et al. [ 23 ] report that single-frame models achieve surprisingly competitive results on video-language tasks, highlighting a static appearance bias. Within the VTG domain, Otani et al. [ 38 ] show that blind baselines without any video input can rival trained models by leveraging annotation distribution patterns. This observation motivated the construction of out-of-distribution evaluation splits [ 50 ] and a line of debiasing methods. Causal inference approaches [ 49 , 36 ] apply backdoor adjustment to mitigate the confounding effect of moment location. Adversarial and augmentation-based strategies [ 39 , 12 , 21 ] either generate bias-conflict samples or perturb the temporal structure to discourage shortcut learning. Chae et al. [ 6]introduce a comprehensive benchmark over seven datasets and analyze annotation bias and query text patterns. Together, these studies indicate that VTG datasets exhibit a wide range of biases across annotation distributions, language patterns, and visual modalities, and that existing models remain susceptible to such shortcuts instead of performing genuine cross-modal grounding. 

## B Further Analysis of the Domain Gap 

B.1 Constructing Seen/Unseen Concept Splits 

For the concept domain gap analysis shown in Figure 3 (a), we partition the QVHighlights (QVH) [ 22 ]test set into seen and unseen concept subsets with respect to the Charades-STA (Cha.) [ 10 ] training 13 distribution. Specifically, we first build a Cha.-centric concept vocabulary C by taking the 50 most frequent content lemmas across all Cha. training queries, after standard lowercasing, lemmatization, and stop-word removal. The resulting set covers the dominant indoor activity, object, and motion vocabulary of Cha. (e.g. glass, walk, sneeze, room, door, cup, shelf, sit, pillow, refrigerator, blanket ). Each QVH test query is then processed with the identical pipeline, and labeled seen if at least one of its lemmas appears in C , otherwise unseen . This rule yields 635 /915 seen/unseen samples out of 1,550 ; to remove the class-size imbalance, we draw a fixed random subset of 600 samples from each group as our final evaluation splits. We use only word-level lemma matching, without expanding to a hand-crafted concept ontology, so that the split reflects lexical familiarity to the Cha.-trained model without additional human bias. 

B.2 Measuring Cross-Domain Visual Similarity 

For the visual domain gap analysis shown in Figure 3 (b), we embed each video as a single global descriptor by passing 20 uniformly sampled 224 ×224 frames through a frozen Qwen2.5-VL-3B [ 3]vision encoder, mean-pooling visual tokens within each frame and then averaging the resulting per-frame vectors. Taking Charades-STA (Cha.) [ 10 ] training videos as the reference distribution, we define a centroid cCha. as the mean of their global descriptors, and characterize each test video by its cosine similarity s(v) = cos (v, cCha. ) to this centroid. While the Cha. training set is internally tight (intra-set similarity 0.894 ± 0.035 over 5,335 videos), QVHighlights (QVH) [ 22 ] test videos drift toward lower similarities ( 0.831 ± 0.061 , range [0.527 , 0.942 ] over 1,519 videos), indicating a measurable train →test visual shift (Figure 6). We sort the QVH test set by s(v) and partition it into five equally sized quantile bins (Figure 6). Reporting performance on the top and bottom quantiles—denoted Visually similar (Q1, s ≥ 0.881 )and Visually dissimilar (Q5, s ≤ 0.789 )—isolates the impact of the visual domain gap on temporal grounding without conflating it with other dataset-specific factors. C.3 Implementation Details 

We build upon Qwen2.5-VL-Instruct [ 3] (7B) as the backbone MLLM, where the LLM decoder has a hidden dimension of D=3584 . The vision encoder processes each 224 ×224 frame into N=64 visual tokens ( 8×8 spatial grid). For the EB Adapter, we set the bottleneck dimension to d=896 , the number of slots to K=4 , and use 8 attention heads with I=3 iterations of GRU-based refinement. We inject the EB Adapter into layers 0–10, while LoRA [ 14 ] (rank 16, α=64 ) is applied to the remaining deeper layers; the vision tower, the LLM’s frozen layers, and the vision–language merger are kept fixed throughout training. The E2V gating module is applied at every EB Adapter layer (0–10) and modulates the slot residual using the cosine similarity between subject/object queries and per-frame mean-pooled visual tokens. The EB Distillation derives pseudo-GT cluster maps by 

K-means clustering DINOv2-large [ 37 ] patch features, and aligns slot-attention maps to these clusters via Hungarian matching with BCE loss at every EB Adapter layer (0–10) with λ =0.1. 

Video sampling and timestamps. We uniformly sample 20 and 60 frames for the models trained on Charades-STA [ 10 ] and QVHighlights [ 22 ], respectively. For cross-domain evaluation, we follow each target dataset’s native protocol and sample 20 frames for Charades-STA, 60 frames for QVHighlights, and 20 frames for DiDeMo [ 13 ]. For Charades-STA training, whose clips are confined to a narrow 0– 30 s range, we further adopt the relative-integer timestamp encoding of Chrono-BLIP [ 33 ], representing each timestamp as an integer in [0, 100 ] relative to clip duration; this mitigates the text bias toward a narrow band of numeric tokens that the dataset’s limited duration range would otherwise induce. 

Gating under partial concept queries. Not every query specifies both a subject and an object: queries such as “a person is dancing” contain a subject but no object. For such samples, we pass the absent concept’s score through as 1 rather than 0: since the per-frame gate is the multiplicative product of the two scores, 1 is its identity element, so the absent concept exerts no influence on gating while the present one continues to drive it—whereas 0 would collapse the gate regardless of the present concept’s signal. 

Optimization. We train for 2 epochs with AdamW [ 32 ] (learning rate 5×10 −5, weight decay 0.1 )under a cosine schedule with 5% linear warmup, using a global batch size of 32 on 8 NVIDIA 3090/4090 GPUs. ## D Additional Experimental Results 

D.1 Analysis on the Behavior of EB Adapter 

We further analyze the internal behavior of EB Adapter by examining its slot attention weights ˆA.Specifically, we measure two complementary properties across all EB Adapter layers (Figure 11): (i) the entropy of each slot’s attention distribution over visual tokens, which quantifies how concentrated each slot is on specific regions, and (ii) the pairwise cosine similarity between slots, which quantifies whether different slots attend to distinct regions. Without our EB Distillation loss, the naïve MLLM visual space yields nearly uniform attention weights (entropy approaching the theoretical uniform bound of 4.2 ) and near-perfect inter-slot cosine similarity ( ≈ 1.0), indicating that all slots collapse to attending to the same diffuse region rather than binding to distinct entities. This confirms that the intermediate visual representation of a pre-trained MLLM does not afford entity-level binding on its own. In contrast, training EB Adapter with our EB Distillation loss drives both metrics down substantially: the entropy drops to around 3.5 and the inter-slot cosine similarity drops to around 0.3 across all layers. This indicates that each slot attends to a sharper, more localized region and that different slots attend to disjoint regions, providing direct evidence that EB Distillation successfully encourages each slot to bind to a distinct entity. 

D.2 Additional Ablation Study 

We provide further ablations on the design choices of EB Distillation and E2V gating in Table 5. 

(a) Effects of EB Adapter insertion layers. We show that inserting the EB Adapter into decoder layers 0–10 yields the best OOD performance. As discussed in Section 4.2, this choice places our EB Adapter in the early layers where cross-frame interactions occur [ 18 ], allowing each slot to capture temporally coherent disentangled semantics. Meanwhile, the mid-to-later layers responsible for vision-language integration [ 18 ] are left to lightweight LoRA [ 14 ] fine-tuning, preventing excessive overfitting to the source domain. 

(b) Effects of token reconstruction design. We demonstrate that reusing the slot attention weights to reconstruct the original token sequence, mentioned in Section 4.2, substantially improves OOD performance (+2.1 points in R1@0.5). This attention reuse design routes VTG adaptation through disentangled entity-level representations and eliminates the additional learnable parameters that could overfit to the source domain. 

(c) Effects of EB Distillation loss scale and placement. We ablate the loss weight λ and the layer indices at which EB Distillation is applied. Across the three loss scales tested under the all-layer setting (0–10), the differences are minor: ID and OOD metrics remain within roughly 1– 2 points 19 across λ ∈ { 1.0, 0.5, 0.1}, indicating that EB Distillation is robust to the choice of loss scale. Our default λ =0.1 achieves the best ID R1@0.5 ( 69 .1 ) and OOD R1@0.7 ( 36 .9 ), while λ =1.0 slightly favors OOD R1@0.5 ( 60 .4 vs. 59 .8 ). Restricting EB Distillation to only the last EB Adapter layer (l=10 ) leads to a small but consistent drop across metrics, suggesting that progressively applying EB Distillation across all EB Adapter layers (0–10) is mildly more effective than aligning only the final layer. 

(d) Effects of visual feature source in E2V gating. We ablate which visual feature is used to compute the per-frame gating score, comparing tokens before down projection, after down projection, and after 

slot-based reconstruction. Features after down projection give the best overall performance, achieving the highest OOD scores (R1@0.5 of 59.8) while remaining competitive on ID. Pre-projection features underperform on both splits, and post-reconstruction features collapse on OOD (R1@0.5 drops to 51.9), as the slot bottleneck discards fine-grained details needed for entity-level scoring. This indicates that E2V gating should be computed from the LLM’s visual stream rather than the bottlenecked slot output. 

(e) Effects of E2V gating applied layers. We ablate whether E2V gating should be applied at every EB Adapter layer (L0–10) or only at the final layer (L10). Applying E2V gating across all EB Adapter layers consistently outperforms the last-layer-only variant on OOD R1@0.5 ( 59 .8 vs. 55 .8 )while maintaining competitive ID performance. This shows that progressively gating the slot residual at every layer better aligns the entity-level representations with query-relevant frames, leading to more robust cross-domain generalization. ## E Limitations and Future Work 

EVIDENT addresses cross-domain VTG under visual distribution shift, with experiments spanning three diverse benchmarks (Charades-STA, QVHighlights, DiDeMo). Extending our analysis to more challenging settings such as long-form videos, egocentric perspectives, or open-vocabulary temporal grounding is a promising direction for future work. While we identify visual domain shift as the dominant failure mode of naïvely fine-tuned MLLMs, jointly addressing other forms of distribution shift, such as temporal granularity or query style, would require complementary advances. We 21 view EVIDENT as a first step toward systematically diagnosing and mitigating distribution shifts in MLLM-based video temporal grounding. 

## F Broader Impact 

EVIDENT improves the robustness of MLLM-based video temporal grounding under domain shift, with potential positive impacts on applications such as video search, content accessibility (e.g., caption alignment for visually impaired users), and video understanding tools for education and media analysis. By promoting entity-grounded reasoning rather than reliance on dataset-specific shortcuts, our framework may also reduce systematic biases inherited from training data, contributing to more reliable model behavior in unseen scenarios. We do not foresee direct negative societal impacts from this work, as it builds on publicly available pre-trained models and datasets and does not enable new generative or surveillance capabilities. Standard considerations applicable to general video understanding research—such as privacy concerns when applying such systems to personal video content—should be observed by downstream users. 22
